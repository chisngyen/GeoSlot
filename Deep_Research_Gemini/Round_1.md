# Architectural Innovations and Knowledge Distillation Paradigms for Lightweight UAV-to-Satellite Cross-View Geo-Localization

## 1. State-of-the-Art (SOTA) Review (2023–2025)

The trajectory of Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) autonomous navigation has increasingly shifted away from absolute reliance on Global Navigation Satellite Systems (GNSS). In complex urban environments, heavily forested areas, or electromagnetically contested zones, GNSS signals are prone to multipath errors, spoofing, and complete degradation.1 To ensure robust flight and operational continuity, the field of visual cross-view geo-localization (CVGL) has emerged as the premier failsafe. The core objective of CVGL is to precisely determine the geographic coordinates of a UAV by matching its onboard camera imagery against a pre-existing, geo-referenced database of satellite or high-altitude aerial maps.3

However, this matching process involves bridging an immense domain gap. Satellite imagery is typically orthographic, captured from a strictly top-down perspective, and characterized by a relatively uniform Ground Sample Distance (GSD). Conversely, UAV imagery is captured at low altitudes with varying pitch, roll, and yaw, resulting in oblique perspectives that expose building facades, dynamic shadows, and severe occlusions that are completely invisible to satellite sensors.2 As the field progresses toward the 2025 benchmark landscape, the dominant architectural paradigm has transitioned from localized Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) to global-context Vision Transformers (ViTs).5 While foundational ViTs achieve unprecedented accuracy, their multi-gigabyte parameter footprints and massive computational demands (often exceeding hundreds of Giga-FLOPs) render them entirely unsuited for the strict size, weight, and power (SWaP) constraints of mobile UAV edge computers.6 Consequently, the most pressing contemporary research vector is the pursuit of high-efficiency, lightweight architectures that maintain state-of-the-art matching performance through advanced model compression techniques, specifically Knowledge Distillation (KD).

### 1.1 Latest Trends in UAV-to-Satellite Matching

The evolution of CVGL methodologies between 2023 and 2025 is defined by a distinct move toward multi-modal feature alignment, generative domain translation, and hierarchical feature aggregation. Early approaches relied heavily on brute-force feature extraction, wherein identical CNN branches processed both satellite and drone images, utilizing a contrastive loss to push matching pairs closer in the latent space. This approach frequently failed due to the geometric discrepancies between the views. To counteract this, recent literature has embraced domain-bridging synthesis. Architectures integrate Perspective Projection Transformation (PPT) modules to geometrically warp oblique UAV images into pseudo-orthographic views.3 This is often paired with Conditional Generative Adversarial Networks (CGANs) that perform style transfer, normalizing the lighting, seasonal variations, and sensor artifacts of the UAV imagery to match the aesthetic distribution of the satellite reference database.3

Another prevailing trend is the exploitation of multi-view and multi-temporal scene matching. Rather than relying on a single, highly ambiguous image frame, contemporary frameworks aggregate information across multiple drone viewpoints. By systematically capturing images at predetermined positions, drones construct a comprehensive multi-view representation encompassing various azimuth angles and altitudes.6 This temporal and spatial fusion ensures that if a distinct landmark is occluded in one frame, it is captured in subsequent frames. Furthermore, the introduction of foundational models pre-trained via self-supervised learning (such as DINOv2) has dramatically elevated the baseline capabilities of feature extractors. These foundational models exhibit remarkable zero-shot robustness against environmental distractors, such as varying weather conditions and illumination shifts.10 Yet, the integration of multi-view processing and foundational extractors exponentially inflates inference latency, creating a profound bottleneck for real-time edge deployment.

### 1.2 Key SOTA Papers on Efficiency and Knowledge Distillation

Addressing the extreme computational overhead of foundational ViTs and multi-branch networks, a subset of recent literature has pioneered edge-deployable CVGL through aggressive lightweight modeling and nuanced distillation paradigms. A critical review of the state-of-the-art reveals several defining frameworks.

The most prominent advancement in this niche is the MobileGeo framework, which explicitly targets resource-efficient cross-view drone geo-localization.6 MobileGeo introduces a mobile-friendly architecture engineered to eliminate the inference overhead associated with complex feature alignment. The framework relies on a dual-phase optimization strategy. During training, it employs a Hierarchical Distillation (HD-CVGL) paradigm.6 This method transfers essential spatial and semantic knowledge from a heavy foundational teacher model into a compact student model. To mitigate the domain discrepancy and data imbalance inherent in cross-view datasets, the authors integrate an Uncertainty-Aware Prediction Alignment (UAPA) mechanism.6 UAPA dynamically balances the learning gradients between the drone and satellite branches by quantifying predictive uncertainty; if a particular cross-view pair is deemed highly ambiguous by the teacher, the distillation penalty is softened, preventing the lightweight student from overfitting to noisy feature mappings.12 During inference, MobileGeo utilizes a Multi-view Selection Refinement Module (MSRM) grounded in mutual information to actively filter out redundant or non-discriminative drone views.6 This selective processing ensures that computational resources are expended only on the most informative frames. The empirical results are substantial: MobileGeo achieves a 4.19% improvement in Average Precision (AP) on the University-1652 benchmark while operating at over five times the efficiency (4.45G FLOPs) and running at 251.5 Frames Per Second (FPS) on an NVIDIA AGX Orin edge device.6

Parallel to distillation efforts, the Global-Local Quadrant Interaction Network (GLQINet) addresses efficiency by structurally redesigning the feature extraction process to minimize redundant attention computations.13 Recognizing that standard ViTs calculate self-attention globally across all image patches—resulting in quadratic complexity—GLQINet partitions feature maps into directional quadrants (horizontal, vertical, diagonal, and anti-diagonal stripes) via a Quadrant Insight Module (QIM).13 This structural prior refines multi-scale spatial representations while preserving intra-class consistency without the need for massive parameter counts. Concurrently, an Integrated Global-Local Attention Module (IGLAM) bridges coarse global context with fine local details by aggregating high-association feature stripes.13 By selectively attending to directional features rather than engaging in dense global attention, GLQINet dramatically reduces computational bloat while achieving state-of-the-art performance on the SUES-200 and University-1652 datasets, demonstrating that mathematically guided feature partitioning can rival the accuracy of unconstrained, parameter-heavy attention mechanisms.13

Another highly influential architecture is the Single-Stream Pyramid Transformer (SSPT).9 Traditional CVGL models utilize siamese or dual-stream networks, wherein the satellite and drone images are processed by separate, heavy backbone branches, effectively doubling the FLOPs required for a single inference pass.9 SSPT circumvents this redundancy by deploying a single-stream architecture. The images are concatenated early in the pipeline, allowing the model's self-attention mechanisms to enrich internal features, followed by cross-attention layers that facilitate direct interaction between the satellite and drone modalities.9 This early-fusion approach effectively filters out irrelevant environmental interference. To further optimize convergence and localization accuracy, the post-processing module utilizes a corner pyramid structure for upsampling and applies a Gaussian weight window.9 The Gaussian kernel naturally emphasizes central local features, smoothing the loss landscape and driving the model to higher accuracy with fewer training epochs. Validated on the UL14 benchmark, the SSPT-384 model improved the Relative Distance Score (RDS) from 76.25% to 84.40%, while demonstrating strong robustness on thermal infrared (TIR) and nighttime extended datasets.9

Finally, the challenge of yaw variance—where the drone's heading differs significantly from the north-aligned satellite map—has driven the development of Rotation-Invariant Knowledge Distillation frameworks.15 Traditional methods handle rotational variance by deploying heavy Steerable CNNs (G-CNNs) that compute rotation-equivariant features, or by augmenting the inference database with dozens of rotated satellite crops, which exponentially increases the retrieval search space.17 Recent distillation paradigms, such as RIFD, bypass this inference overhead by leveraging Vision-Language Models (VLMs) as teachers. The heavy teacher model is trained to recognize geometric stability, distilling robust rotational knowledge into a lightweight detector during the training phase.16 The student model learns to map rotated inputs to a normalized, rotation-invariant feature space. Consequently, during edge inference, the lightweight model produces orientation-agnostic descriptors without executing complex equivariant convolutions, maintaining high retrieval speed.16

### 1.3 Identification of Current Limitations and "Gaps"

Despite these sophisticated architectures, deploying hyper-compressed models on physical UAVs while maintaining the rigorous Recall@1 performance required for autonomous navigation remains an unsolved paradigm. A critical analysis of the current literature exposes several fundamental gaps that trigger severe performance degradation in lightweight models.

The primary limitation is the occurrence of "Capacity Collapse" during extreme knowledge distillation.21 When attempting to distill the vast, high-dimensional topological manifold of a 300-million parameter ViT into a 5-million parameter mobile CNN (e.g., MobileNetV3), the student physically lacks the channel depth and receptive capacity to absorb the teacher's nuanced semantic granularity. Standard logit-level distillation or pixel-wise feature map alignment forces the lightweight model into an optimization bottleneck. It attempts to replicate the exact numerical activations of the teacher rather than the underlying structural logic. This results in feature collapse, where the student network produces nearly identical, non-discriminative embeddings for distinct geographic locations, causing the Recall@1 metric to plummet during dense urban matching.

A secondary, equally critical gap is the Cross-Resolution Information Loss.22 Satellite imagery possesses a fixed Ground Sample Distance (GSD), generally rendering a low-resolution, macroscopic view of building footprints and road networks. UAVs, however, operate at highly variable altitudes (e.g., 50 meters to 300 meters), capturing high-frequency micro-textures such as roof tiles, individual vehicles, and foliage.24 Existing KD frameworks generally force the student and teacher to operate on identically scaled inputs, often downsampling the drone imagery to match the satellite scale. This naive spatial downsampling destroys the micro-geometric structures necessary for fine-grained localization.22 Furthermore, when a lightweight student is trained on uncompressed high-resolution inputs, it tends to overfit to transient high-frequency noise (e.g., a specific parked car) rather than the permanent low-frequency geometry (e.g., the building shape). Because the student lacks the multi-head attention mechanisms required to separate transient noise from permanent structure, its matching accuracy degrades significantly when environmental conditions change.

Lastly, the disparity between multi-view theoretical performance and edge-compute reality represents a significant deployment hurdle. While multi-view fusion modules theoretically improve localization by aggregating features across consecutive drone frames, they impose a linear multiplier on computational overhead.7 If a lightweight model requiring 2 GFLOPs per frame processes a temporal window of 5 frames, the effective inference cost spikes to 10 GFLOPs per localization update. This negates the foundational purpose of edge deployment. Current SOTA architectures lack efficient temporal propagation mechanisms; they re-extract deep spatial features from scratch for every frame rather than updating a probabilistic localization state sequentially. Bridging these gaps requires a fundamental rethinking of how knowledge is parsed, how resolutions are reconciled, and how temporal data is aggregated within strict FLOP constraints.

2. 3 Novel Ideas for ACM Multimedia

To directly address the limitations of capacity collapse, cross-resolution degradation, and temporal computational overhead, the following three distinct research architectures are proposed. These concepts are meticulously engineered to align with the core themes of the ACM Multimedia conference—specifically emphasizing multimedia systems, intelligent immersification, and the integration of generative AI within stringent hardware constraints.26

### Idea 1: Scale-Aware Frequency-Domain Knowledge Distillation for Cross-Resolution UAV-Satellite Matching

Title:Bridging the Altitude Gap: Frequency-Aware Cross-Resolution Knowledge Distillation for Ultra-Lightweight UAV Geo-Localization

The "Hook" / Novelty: A persistent failure mode in edge-deployed cross-view geo-localization is the spatial resolution mismatch. Satellites capture low-resolution, top-down macroscopic semantics, whereas UAVs capture high-resolution, oblique micro-details.22 Reviewers in the ACM Multimedia systems track will highly value this proposition because it completely shifts the Knowledge Distillation paradigm from the spatial domain to the spectral (frequency) domain.29 High-frequency visual data (such as temporary parked cars, sharp leaf textures, and transient shadows) often acts as noise when matching against outdated satellite maps. Conversely, low-frequency data contains the invariant structural geometries—such as building footprints, primary road arteries, and topological layouts—that remain constant across both views.31 By utilizing a Discrete Wavelet Transform (DWT), this method distills only the structurally invariant low-frequency representations from a massive teacher network to a lightweight student, effectively teaching the small model to prioritize geometric layout over transient high-resolution textural noise.

Proposed Architecture:

The framework establishes an asymmetric Teacher-Student distillation pipeline that operates dynamically across resolutions.

* Teacher Model: A heavy, multi-scale Vision Transformer (e.g., Swin-Transformer Base or ViT-Large) that is fully pre-trained on uncompressed, high-resolution cross-view pairs to generate ideal structural embeddings.21
* Student Model: An ultra-lightweight hybrid architecture, such as FastViT or a modified MobileViT, designed specifically for low-latency edge deployment.13
* Distillation Mechanism: The core innovation is the Wavelet-based Cross-Resolution Distillation Module. During the training phase, the intermediate spatial feature maps of both the teacher (processing high-resolution inputs) and the student (processing heavily downsampled, low-resolution inputs) are projected into the frequency domain using a 2D Haar Discrete Wavelet Transform (DWT).30 The DWT splits the spatial features into four distinct frequency subbands: ![]() (low-low), ![]() (low-high), ![]() (high-low), and ![]() (high-high).

### Idea 2: Rotation-Equivariant Relational Distillation with Temporal Markov Propagation

Title:Spin and Track: Rotation-Equivariant Relational Distillation with Temporal Markov Propagation for Continuous UAV Map Matching

The "Hook" / Novelty: UAVs are inherently dynamic platforms that constantly alter their yaw (heading) during flight to track targets or navigate waypoints. This creates massive rotational misalignment against north-aligned satellite maps.15 The current SOTA solution requires augmenting the retrieval database with dozens of rotated satellite crops (e.g., 36 rotations at 10-degree increments), which bloats the database size by 36x and severely bottlenecks the K-Nearest Neighbor (k-NN) search time during inference.35 Alternatively, deploying rotation-equivariant G-CNNs on the drone introduces prohibitive computational drag.19 This paper introduces a paradigm shift: distilling rotation-invariant relational knowledge into a standard, highly optimized CNN, and fusing it with a Temporal Markov formulation that utilizes the continuous video stream to propagate localization confidence over time.36 This eliminates the need for rotational gallery augmentation and redundant single-frame feature extraction, directly addressing ACM Multimedia's focus on seamless multimedia systems and continuous data stream processing.

Proposed Architecture:

* Teacher Model: A heavy Rotation-Equivariant network (e.g., ReDet or an ![]()-equivariant Steerable CNN) that mathematically guarantees orientation-agnostic feature extraction regardless of input yaw.37
* Student Model: A highly optimized, standard architecture such as MobileNetV3 or EfficientNet-Lite, which lacks inherent rotational equivariance but excels in hardware-accelerated environments (e.g., INT8 TensorRT execution).
* Distillation Mechanism: Because the student lacks the physical convolution filters to replicate equivariant feature maps pixel-by-pixel, standard feature map alignment will fail. Instead, the framework employs Relational Knowledge Distillation (RKD).16 A batch of drone images, augmented through continuous 360-degree rotations, is passed through both networks. The teacher generates identical, stable embeddings for all rotated variants. The distillation process utilizes an angular and distance-wise triplet loss to penalize the student if the geometric relationships (the distance and angle between the embeddings of the rotated variants) diverge from the teacher's relational graph. This forces the MobileNetV3 to construct a latent manifold that approximates rotational invariance.16
* Temporal Markov Alignment: During inference, instead of performing an exhaustive, full-database retrieval for every video frame, the system initializes a probability grid over the satellite map. The lightweight student extracts features from the current frame to update the posterior probabilities of the previous frame using a Markov Localization framework, guided by the drone's internal IMU odometry.5

Efficiency Claim:

This framework eradicates the 36x computational and memory penalty incurred during the vector similarity search phase, as the student inherently outputs yaw-agnostic descriptors. Furthermore, because the student network relies on standard depthwise-separable convolutions rather than complex steerable filters, it achieves sub-5ms latency on edge hardware. By leveraging temporal Markov updates, the necessity for full global database querying is reduced by approximately 80%. The system effectively transfers the massive compute burden from global image retrieval to lightweight local probability tracking, achieving seamless video-rate localization.

### Idea 3: Prompt-Guided Cross-View Distillation: Transferring Foundation Model Priors to Edge-Deployable CNNs

Title:Foundation Priors at the Edge: Prompt-Guided Semantic Distillation for Zero-Shot Environmental Adaptation in Drone Geo-Localization

The "Hook" / Novelty: The ACM Multimedia community places a high premium on the innovative application of Generative AI and multi-modal foundation models.26 Foundational vision models, such as DINOv2 and the Segment Anything Model (SAM), exhibit unparalleled zero-shot semantic understanding. They are inherently resilient to environmental domain shifts, seamlessly matching snowy winter drone footage to summer satellite maps.10 However, deploying a model with hundreds of millions of parameters on a drone is a physical impossibility. This research proposes "Prompt-Guided Semantic Distillation." In this framework, foundation models are utilized entirely offline to generate highly accurate semantic prompt masks. These masks act as spatial attention priors, physically guiding the distillation process of a lightweight CNN, teaching the small model to focus exclusively on invariant geometric structures while actively ignoring transient environmental artifacts.

Proposed Architecture:

* Teacher Model: A frozen DINOv2 ViT-Large model, augmented offline with SAM to generate precise semantic segmentation boundaries.10
* Assistant Network: To bridge the immense capacity gap between the foundational teacher and the mobile student, a medium-tier network (e.g., ResNet-50) is used as an intermediate knowledge proxy.21
* Student Model: An ultra-efficient edge network, such as MobileNetV3-Small.
* Distillation Mechanism: During the offline training phase, SAM processes the satellite and drone images, generating explicit semantic masks categorizing pixels into "buildings", "road infrastructure", "vehicles", and "vegetation".39 A lightweight cross-attention module injects these semantic masks into the distillation pipeline. Efficiency Claim: By utilizing prompt-guided masks during training, the student network internalizes the environmental robustness and selective attention of a 300-million parameter foundational model while operating with fewer than 5 million parameters. This architectural strategy results in a 60x reduction in model footprint and a near 98% reduction in memory bandwidth utilization.39 It enables the deployment of highly robust, weather-invariant matching algorithms on the smallest tier of UAV companion computers (e.g., Raspberry Pi 4 equipped with a Coral Edge TPU), establishing a new frontier for SWaP-constrained multimedia retrieval.

### 3.3 Key Evaluation Metrics

The evaluation must concurrently report on visual matching fidelity and hardware-level execution costs.

Retrieval Accuracy Metrics:

* Recall@K (R@1, R@5, R@10): The primary and most critical metric indicating the percentage of queries where the true corresponding satellite image is retrieved within the top ![]() ranked results. R@1 is the paramount indicator of practical usability for autonomous navigation, as the drone relies on the absolute top match for coordinate updates.2
* Average Precision (AP): Measures the precision of the ranked retrieval list, evaluating the model's ability to rank the true match significantly above all geometrically similar distractors.6
* Relative Distance Score (RDS) & Meter-level Accuracy (MA): Utilized to quantify the physical localization error limits in physical space (e.g., successful localization within a 3m, 5m, or 20m radius of the ground truth).9

Computational Efficiency & Systems Metrics:

* FLOPs (Floating Point Operations): Measured in Giga-FLOPs (GFLOPs) to quantify computational algorithmic complexity independent of specific hardware implementations.6
* Number of Parameters (Params): Measured in millions (M) to indicate the memory footprint and storage requirements of the compiled model.
* Inference Time & FPS (Frames Per Second): Theoretical FLOPs frequently fail to translate linearly to latency due to memory bandwidth bottlenecks and operator inefficiencies. Therefore, inference must be strictly profiled on target edge hardware. The standard benchmarking devices will be the NVIDIA Jetson AGX Orin (utilizing FP16 precision via TensorRT) and the older NVIDIA Jetson TX2 to unequivocally prove true mobile viability and backwards compatibility.6

4. Potential Pitfalls & Solutions

Engineering highly compressed, distillation-dependent neural networks for an environment as geometrically chaotic and semantically dense as UAV-to-Satellite map matching introduces a multitude of systemic and theoretical pitfalls. Anticipating these challenges and proactively architecting mathematical fail-safes is critical for ensuring model convergence, preventing feature collapse, and guaranteeing real-world applicability.

### 4.1 The Capacity Gap and Feature Space Collapse

The Pitfall: The most severe risk in Knowledge Distillation occurs when transferring knowledge from a massive Vision Transformer (e.g., DINOv2 containing over 300 million parameters) directly into an ultra-lightweight mobile CNN (e.g., MobileNetV3 containing roughly 5 million parameters).21 The high-dimensional embedding space of the ViT teacher contains highly subtle, multi-head attention-driven topological structures. The student physically lacks the channel depth, non-linear complexity, and spatial receptive field to mimic these activations. Consequently, enforcing a strict pixel-to-pixel Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss on the feature vectors forces the student's gradient updates to oscillate wildly. This frequently results in "feature space collapse," an optimization failure where the student network gives up and predicts identical, flattened representations for entirely varying geographies, rendering K-NN retrieval impossible.

The Solution: To circumvent capacity collapse, the architecture must implement a decoupled, multi-stage distillation process.6

1. Assistant Networks (Proxy Distillation): Introduce an intermediate network (e.g., a ResNet-50) to bridge the dimensionality step-down. The knowledge is transferred progressively: first from the massive ViT to the ResNet-50, and subsequently from the ResNet-50 to the MobileNetV3. This progressive staging smooths the loss landscape and prevents optimization failure.21
2. Relational Knowledge Distillation (RKD): Instead of attempting to map tensor-to-tensor numerical values, the loss function should measure the angle and Euclidean distance between specific feature clusters within a batch (e.g., building triplets). The student is trained to replicate the relational geometric graph of the teacher’s embedding space, rather than the absolute coordinate values of the high-dimensional tensors.16

### 4.2 Severe Spatial Misalignment and Oblique Viewpoint Shifts

The Pitfall: Standard feature-map distillation inherently assumes strict spatial correspondence between the teacher and student inputs. However, a drone capturing an image at a 45-degree oblique pitch visualizes extensive building facades, whereas the satellite sensor capturing a top-down view registers only flat rooftops.3 If the teacher network processes the satellite image and the student processes the drone image, forcing a spatial ![]() map distillation will heavily penalize the student for extracting facade data. This forces the student to ignore the very visual cues it needs to navigate, inherently degrading its cross-view capability and causing immediate drops in Recall@1.

The Solution:

1. Uncertainty-Aware Prediction Alignment (UAPA): Leveraging the UAPA mechanism 6, the framework dynamically balances the learning gradient based on predictive uncertainty. If the teacher network identifies a spatial region as highly uncertain due to spatial misalignment (e.g., a heavily shadowed facade or an occluded alleyway), the distillation loss weight for that specific spatial grid is severely attenuated.33
2. Deformable Convolutions & Logit-Level KD: The architecture should shift focus away from rigid spatial feature distillation toward Logit-level KD (utilizing Kullback-Leibler divergence on the final softened output probability distributions).7 This is combined with the deployment of deformable convolutions in the student's terminal layers. Deformable convolutions allow the student to flexibly warp its spatial receptive field, dynamically stretching to match the orthographic footprint required by the satellite map without requiring rigid pixel alignment.38

### 4.3 Extreme Resolution Discrepancy and Scale Variance

The Pitfall: The empirical data from the SUES-200 dataset demonstrates that UAVs operate at highly variable altitudes, ranging from 150m to 300m.24 A lightweight network processing a low-altitude UAV image will encounter a massive influx of high-frequency textures (e.g., individual leaves on trees, roof tiles, distinct vehicle shapes) that fundamentally do not exist in the 0.5m/pixel low-resolution satellite reference map. This resolution gap causes the low-capacity student network to overfit to transient high-frequency noise, destroying its ability to generalize across different flight altitudes.22

The Solution: The implementation of a Cross-Resolution Knowledge Distillation (CRKD) framework operating within the spectral domain is mandatory.22 As outlined in Idea 1, applying a 2D Discrete Wavelet Transform (DWT) to separate the intermediate feature maps into high and low-frequency subbands isolates the noise.30 By enforcing the KD loss exclusively on the low-frequency (![]()) structural subbands, the network becomes mathematically blind to the high-frequency textural discrepancies that plague cross-resolution matching. This explicitly forces the student to allocate its limited parameter budget purely toward understanding macroscopic geometries (road layouts, building outlines, geographic topology) that remain resolvable regardless of the drone's operational altitude.

### 4.4 Data Imbalance and Massive Gallery Distractors

The Pitfall: In physical real-world deployment, the satellite map database does not consist of a few hundred images; it contains millions of cropped geometric tiles. As demonstrated in the University-160kWX dataset 10, the presence of massive distractor sets—satellite images containing semantically identical layouts, such as sprawling suburban housing developments with identical roof structures—causes catastrophic false-positive rates in lightweight models that lack the deep self-attention mechanisms required to discern micro-variations.

The Solution:

1. Hard Negative Mining with InfoNCE: During the KD training phase, the standard contrastive loss must be aggressively augmented with a hard negative mining strategy. The batch construction must deliberately and continuously pair the target building query with visually identical distractor buildings from other geographic regions. This forces the student's metric learning objective to isolate and amplify the subtle geometric variations that separate true positives from highly correlated distractors.
2. Mutual Information-Based View Refinement: To prevent the model from querying the massive database with ambiguous frames, the system must integrate an inference-time Multi-view Selection Refinement Module (MSRM).6 By exploiting the mutual information across a short temporal buffer of incoming drone frames, the drone’s edge computer can actively score the "uniqueness" of a frame. The system will then filter out redundant or ambiguous views, ensuring that only the most highly discriminative, feature-rich frames are queried against the distractor-heavy satellite database, preserving both accuracy and K-NN search efficiency.6
